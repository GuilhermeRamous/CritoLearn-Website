History

From classical antiquity, and perhaps even before, people use cryptography to convey secret messages. The idea was simple: I have a method or key that I use to "shuffle" a message and only those who know this method or message would be able to make this message readable again.

The major problem with this approach is that both the sender and receiver have to know the encryption key. For example, if I - that I am in SP - wanted to send a message encrypted by me to the Lambda3 RJ people now, I would have to find a safe way to send them the key I used to encrypt the text. How could I send this key securely? I could not.

In the 1970s two computer scientists working at MIT, Ronald Rivest and Adi Shamir, were engaged in creating an efficient asymmetric key cryptography method. They were aided by the mathematician Leonard Adleman, who validated their ideas from the mathematical point of view. In 1977 all three registered the RSA method patent.

How it works??

This is the legal part. With RSA I have two keys: one public and one private. The public key, as the name says, I can distribute freely; anyone can use my public key to encrypt a message to me. However, it is only possible to decrypt the message using my private key, which I keep in secret.

Using the idea of the previous example, for Lambda SP to send a message to Lambda RJ, it is enough that SP encrypts the message with the public key of Lambda RJ and sends it. Only RJ will be able to read the message because only they have the private key needed to decrypt the message.

The RSA was built on one of the most classical areas of mathematics, the Theory of Numbers. It relies on the difficulty of factoring a number into its prime components. First let's remember that a prime number is a number that can only be divided by itself and by 1 (in an exact division, without broken numbers); second, we have to remember how to find the prime factors of a number.

According to the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic every positive integer greater than 1 can be decomposed uniquely into a product of prime numbers, for example:

26 = 2 * 13

44 = 2 * 2 * 11

Fatorar números pequenos é algo simples, mas fatorar números grandes é bem difícil e demorado, pois este é um problema que não pode ser resolvido em um tempo polinomial determinístico, ou falando de forma bem simplificada, não há uma fórmula para isto.

And how does RSA use all this? Public and private keys are generated based on the multiplication of two prime numbers. The result of this multiplication will be public, but if the number is large enough, factoring this number to discover the primes we have multiplied to form it may take years.

It is this particularity that comes RSA security. Actually it is not impossible to break the RSA encryption, but how to do this would take some good years or decades, the idea becomes impracticable.
